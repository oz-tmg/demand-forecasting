## What this project is (and is not)

`demand-sim` is a **ground-truth demand-curve simulator and ideal-dataset
generator** for demand-forecasting and demand-estimation research. It simulates
pricing experiments that trace out demand curves (Uber / ZipRecruiter style),
emits forecasting-ready daily panels (Amazon / Chronos style), and — because it
generated the truth — **grades elasticity and forecasting estimators on bias,
RMSE, and CI coverage**, which no real dataset can do.

It **is**:
- A methods testbed: score RCT cell means, RD, DML, IV, logit MLE, and structural
  estimators against known elasticities, per population segment.
- An experiment-design lab: pick randomization units, window lengths, and sample
  sizes by Monte Carlo power before spending real traffic.
- An ideal forecasting dataset: panels with known-future covariates and
  controllable censoring, plus *uncensored true demand* as an oracle target.
- A portfolio / study piece built alongside the demand-estimation literature and
  the class of problems Amazon's Demand Forecasting Science team works on.
- Fully synthetic and seed-reproducible — no real or client data anywhere.

It **is not**:
- A client engagement or a deliverable owed to anyone.
- A production forecasting service or a reusable platform component.
- A claim to have real elasticities for any real market — every number is
  generated by a DGP we chose.

## Relationship to the vertical and portfolio

This project sits in the **Economics** vertical (`verticals/economics/`) alongside
`consumer_baskets`. It leans on the vertical's econometric standards
(`verticals/economics/CLAUDE.md`) — real vs. nominal discipline, standard errors
and significance, stationarity checks, DiD/IV causal hygiene — and applies them to
a *simulated* setting where the causal answer is known.

Its portfolio job is to demonstrate quantitative-economics and causal-inference
capability: it shows the failure modes of naive elasticity estimation and the
identification strategies that fix them, on data where the ground truth is
inspectable. If a genuinely reusable asset emerges (e.g. the estimator-scoring
harness), note it as a candidate to generalize — a separate, deliberate decision,
not a silent scope expansion.

## Project summary

**Title:** Demand Curve Simulator & Ideal Forecasting Dataset Generator (`demand-sim`)
**Purpose:** (a) simulate pricing experiments capable of tracing a demand curve,
(b) emit an "ideal dataset" for demand-forecasting research, and (c) grade
elasticity estimators against known truth (bias / RMSE / coverage).
**Status:** Phases 1–5 implemented (see roadmap); Phase 5 = the forecasting
benchmark (docs/09). R Markdown estimator walk-throughs live in `analysis/`.
Holidays + GluonTS/Chronos export, CUPED, sequential bounds, DML scorecard rows,
and quantile loss pending.
**Owner:** Alex Oswald
**Language:** Python primary; R snippets as reference implementations.

One-paragraph pitch: every method for learning a demand curve rests on assumptions
you cannot verify in the field — randomization integrity, no interference,
unconfoundedness, instrument validity. A simulator inverts the problem: *you* set
the truth, then measure exactly how each method fails when its assumptions break.
The observable view is what a real firm logs; the oracle view (true WTP, true
demand, censoring amount, true elasticity) exists solely for scoring.

## Portfolio thesis

The project is meant to demonstrate that Savepoint / Alex can:
- Reason about **identification**, not just fit models — show when OLS elasticity
  is biased and by how much, and which design (RCT / RD / IV / DML / structural)
  recovers the truth.
- Treat **censoring as first-class** — sales are not demand once inventory exists;
  forecasting on censored sales under-forecasts exactly the fast movers that matter.
- Design experiments that actually identify a curve — randomization unit, power,
  guardrails (SRM, balance, dual-exposure, A/A), interference.
- Communicate a technical result honestly, with the estimator's bias shown as a
  function of the mechanism strength — a plot no real dataset can produce.

If a piece of work does not advance one of these claims, cut it.

## Audience

- **Data science / econometrics reader:** the rigor — DGP, identification strategy,
  estimator scoring against ground truth, coverage.
- **Hiring managers / demand-science teams (Amazon-style):** evidence of fluency

- **General analytics community:** a legible demonstration of *why heterogeneity
  and censoring are the phenomenon, not a nuisance.*

## Scope

In scope:
- Random-utility population model (finite mixture of segments) as the generative
  ground truth; logit and WTP-threshold choice models.
- Session-level offers (price shown + buy/no-buy, including non-buyers) as the unit
  of record; daily sales panels derived from sessions.
- NHPP arrivals with trend / weekly / annual seasonality and promos; (s, S)
  inventory with lead time producing realistic stockout censoring.
- Experiment module: user / session / switchback / geo assignment, surge-RD,
  guardrails, analytic + Monte Carlo power, design × estimator scorecard.
- Endogenous pricing, reference prices / loss aversion, substitution, demand
  shifting, and cross-arm interference — each with a strength dial and known truth.
- Estimator scoring: cell means, arc elasticity, logit MLE, OLS, 2SLS, control
  function, local-linear RD, against oracle elasticities/contrasts.

Out of scope (non-goals):
- Real or client data of any kind.
- A production data pipeline, orchestration, or a hosted service.
- Claiming the simulated elasticities describe any real market.
- Full BLP estimation and deep/foundation forecasting models are *targets/hooks*,
  not yet load-bearing (PyBLP, GluonTS/DeepAR, Chronos export are pending).

## Data approach

- **Source:** entirely synthetic, generated by the simulator; deterministic under a
  fixed seed (default 42).
- **Why synthetic:** the whole point is an *oracle* — known true demand,
  elasticities, and censoring — so every estimator can be graded honestly. A real
  dataset cannot expose the truth its methods are trying to recover.
- **Observable vs. oracle split:** every table exists in two views. Estimators only
  ever see the observable view (`out["sessions"]`, `fact_sales_daily.units_sold`);
  truth (`sessions_oracle`, `wtp_true`, `purchase_prob_true`, `units_demanded`,
  `lost_sales`, `true_elasticity()`, `ground_truth_curves`) is scoring-only.
- **Schema:** `dim_segment`, `dim_product`, `fact_session`, `fact_sales_daily`,
  `fact_experiment`, `ground_truth_curves` (full column dictionary in `docs/00_spec.md`).

## Methodology

- **Ground truth is generative, estimation is downstream.** Demand comes from the
  random-utility population model (the object BLP-style structural models *assume*).
  Aggregate demand is the share-weighted segment mixture — deliberately *not*
  constant-elasticity, so estimator misspecification can be tested.
- **Estimators under test** (Python primary, R reference — see `docs/03`):
  log-log OLS / FE panels, binary logit / GLM, mixed logit, DML (DoubleML /
  EconML), IV / 2SLS, regression discontinuity (`rdrobust`), BLP (`PyBLP`, Python
  only), Tobit / censored, probabilistic forecasting (GluonTS / Chronos /
  `statsforecast`), power analysis.
- **Scoring:** `metrics.py` auto-generates bias / RMSE / CI-coverage reports vs.
  the oracle; `power.py` produces Monte Carlo power and a design scorecard;
  guardrails (SRM, covariate balance, dual-exposure, A/A battery) run automatically.
- **Validation / acceptance (Phase 1):** downward-sloping demand for all valid
  configs; cell-mean conversion recovers P(p) at ≈95% coverage with N ≥ 100k
  randomized sessions; logit MLE recovers pooled `price_coef` within ±5% at
  N = 250k (single segment) and shows the *expected, documented* aggregation bias
  under mixtures; arc elasticity matches truth at cell midpoints within noise;
  deterministic under fixed seed.

## Important decisions made

| Area | Decision | Rationale | Revisit if |
|---|---|---|---|
| Framing | Ground truth is generative; every estimator is scored against parameters we set. | Only a simulator can grade methods on bias/coverage; that is the project's reason to exist. | Never — this is the founding principle. |
| Views | Strict observable vs. oracle split; estimators never see the oracle. | Prevents leakage; mirrors what a real firm actually logs. | Never. |
| Unit of record | Sessions (offer = price + buy/no-buy, incl. non-buyers); panels are derived. | The Uber/ZipRecruiter lesson: sales-only data can't identify a curve. | A use case needs only aggregate panels. |
| Censoring | First-class: `units_sold = min(units_demanded, on_hand)`, truth retained. | Sales ≠ demand; censored training data under-forecasts fast movers (the Amazon OOS problem). | Never. |
| Language | Python primary; R as reference snippets. | Simulator + Amazon-style stack are Python; BLP and deep forecasting are Python-only. | R becomes required for a specific estimator. |
| Heterogeneity | Population is a finite mixture of segments; aggregate demand is not constant-elasticity. | Lets the sim expose aggregation bias in pooled estimators. | Never — heterogeneity is the phenomenon. |
| Reproducibility | Deterministic under a fixed seed (default 42). | Acceptance criterion; portfolio credibility. | Never. |
| Phase 2 price term | `arrivals.py` carries the Phase 1 demand curve as a constant-elasticity multiplier per series; the full random-utility model is a documented one-function swap. | Phase 2 was built self-contained before session→panel unification; keeps the panel generator simple. | The session→panel unification lands. |
| Interference benchmark | Capacity is a fixed physical resource (`pool_capacity` computed once, shared across counterfactual worlds); the estimand is the global-rollout contrast. | Recomputing capacity per world lets the resource move with treatment — exactly what interference forbids. | Never. |
| Waiting detector | Test revisit sessions' *previous* prices vs the served distribution, not cheap- vs dear-cell buyer priors. | The naive comparison inverts under WTP heterogeneity (deferrers are high-WTP and buy anywhere); prev-price is selection-proof. | Organic-revisit structure changes. |
| Phase 5 stack | Core statsmodels only (seasonal naive, ETS, Poisson GLM x censoring treatments); GluonTS/Chronos stay pending hooks. | The benchmark is about censoring handling, not architecture; keeps quickstart deps minimal. | Probabilistic forecasting lands. |
| R walk-throughs | `analysis/*.Rmd` are raw, section-by-section reference implementations following docs/03's own R snippets; datasets exported by a seeded CLI. | Reviewability over abstraction (explicit request); R stays reference-only per the language decision. | R becomes load-bearing. |
